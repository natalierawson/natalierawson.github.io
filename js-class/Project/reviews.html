The King and I

Music and Lyrics: Richard Rodgers and Oscar Hammerstein II

While this show is a standard, the 1977 cast recording is arguably the best representative of why. Towers is a plucky, yet tuneful Anna Leonowens. Brynner is at his most comfortable in the role of the King, having been given over 20 years to grow into it after originating it in the early 1950s. The supporting cast is also very strong, but since I'm not being graded on my knowledge musical theater history and I can't remember who was cast as what, I'll just say that the lady playing Tuptim and the man playing her lover deliver their duets stunningly, especially "I Have Dreamed". Hello, Dolly!

Music and Lyrics: Jerry Hermann

Nostalgia reigns on this pick. While many will cite Carol Channing as the definitive Dolly, I will always hold affection for the Babs protrayal. The 1969 movie adaptation of "Hello, Dolly!" was the catalyst for my love of musical theater, so I'm willing to let many things slide here. The one issue I am unwilling to let slide in the name of nostalgia is that of the lack of any chemistry between Matthau and Streisand. There must be some production that can show why the vivacious Dolly Levi and the unbearably drab and curmudgeonly Horace Vandergelder work as a couple because this movie fails to deliver. The two leads were at war behind the scenes and it shows Similarly, though Michael Crawford lacks the singing chops, his Cornelius Hackle is nothing short of adorkable. Phantom (An American Sensation? IDK.)

Music and Lyrics: Maury Yeston and Arthur Kopit

Easily the more obscure of the two Phantom of the Opera shows conceived in the 80s. Blown away by the success of the Webber version, the Yeston version was restricted to an Off-Broadway run (and a mini-series sans music). This by no means means it was the inferior of the two shows, though. Yeston's Phantom, both the character and the work, radiates more warmth than their rock opera counterparts. Shrek: The Musical

Music and Lyrics: Jeanine Tesori

In spite of it sounding like a blatant "Movie: The Musical" sell-out, Shrek manages to deliver. With a cast headed by Brian d'Arcy James and the incomparable Sutton Foster, Shrek: The Musical is able to delve a bit deeper into the collective stories of the title ogre and others from the Dreamworks movie.
